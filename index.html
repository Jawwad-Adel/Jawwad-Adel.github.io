Product

We Are a Small Team of Entrepreneurs Pioneering the Industry’s First all Automatic Machine Learning Resistor Sorter.

The Product

Futility is a product which not only separates used resistors from foreign components, it also sorts them by value, dramatically reducing labour time and cost. The product sorts and allows for the reuse of resistors which otherwise are thrown out, making Futility a cost, effort and time-saving tool.

Market

In Futility, we have built a product which fills a gap in the market and which we are sure will make radical improvements to a large number of customers in business and industry. Futility supplies the great existing demand from educational facilities and R&D companies, such as Imperial Colleague London EEE department, but we believe its market could be even bigger. The current prototype is projected to cost £126.53, representing real value for money when one considers the savings it will be bring about in terms of labour or un-recycled materials. Due to the beautiful simplicity of the design, Futility is a product that is ready for scalability suggesting that the price will drop the price significantly in the future.

Machine Learning

Indeed, the Futility design is unique and superior to alternative solutions due to the product’s computer vision features. This gives the product the unique advantage that it can identify any component and reject non-resistor components such as capacitors and loose wires.

EEE Project Lab Manager Victor Boddy stated "a product which is highly useful in the lab" .

Project Futility

Lead and Camera Setup

The Pi camera is placed directly above a moving conveyor belt where it can detect the passing resistors. The LED creates a more suitable condition for detection. After detection, a signal is sent to the dynamic resistor containers.

  • Date: January 2017

Project Futility

Dynamic Resistor Container

The containers which store the sorted resistors is capable of rotary motion using stepper motors. The signal sent by the detection phase instructs the stepper motor on the rotation it must perform for the resistor to drop to the correct wedge. The wedges are also detachable to allow for easier unloading of the sorted resistors.

  • Date: January 2017
  • Client: Explore
  • Category: Graphic Design

Project Futility

Assembly

The final design involves the user manually loading resistors onto the conveyor belt which is powered by a stepper motor these are then sorted into their allocated bins by the identification of the bands using computer vision. The resistors are dropped into one of twelve wedges which will sort for one order of magnitude of the E12 resistor series.

Question a

What Makes a Good Website?

A good website should present its information in a clear, concise and uncluttered way. Negative space prevents the page from being too cluttered. Typography is used to make the page both clear and visually appealing. The home page should provide clear navigation through the content of the website. Multimedia material and visuals should also be used to engage the viewer and make clear certain ideas, concepts and products without the need for too much text. Fast-loading pages are also an essential feature of a good website, as these ensure the reader is not bored or frustrated by the speed at which the content appears. The website should have a strong and clear message making it evident what the product is and what it does.

What is accessibility and what considerations you should make for it?

A website should be as accessible as possible to people who are disabled. This includes provision for the blind and deaf. Assistive technologies should be used where possible, including screen readers that vocalise the text on each web page, and alt tags which show the content being hovered over by the cursor. Smart colour coding is also a beneficial idea for the visually impaired or the colour blind.
